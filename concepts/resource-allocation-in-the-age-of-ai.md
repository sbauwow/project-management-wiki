# Resource Allocation in the Age of AI

Traditional project management assumes execution labor is scarce. AI changes that. Drafting, analysis, coding, summarization, and research become cheaper. But other resources stay scarce or become even more important.

## What is still scarce
- leadership attention
- taste and judgment
- review capacity
- access to production systems and real users
- decision rights
- integration bandwidth
- trust

## Core shift

The bottleneck moves from doing work to governing work.

That means resource allocation should optimize not just for labor hours, but for:
- decision bandwidth
- review throughput
- integration effort
- risk-adjusted autonomy
- context maintenance cost

## New allocation questions
- where should humans spend deep judgment?
- what work can agents do with low supervision?
- where is review becoming overloaded?
- which workstreams create reusable leverage vs one-off output?
- where are we underinvesting in prompts, tooling, or templates that would compound?

## Common mistake

Organizations see faster execution and respond by opening too many simultaneous initiatives. The result is not acceleration. It is coordination debt.

## Good heuristic

Allocate humans to:
- problem framing
- prioritization
- approvals
- integration
- exception handling
- quality bar setting

Allocate agents to:
- drafting
- repetitive analysis
- scaffolding
- first-pass research
- test generation
- data transformation
- low-risk execution

## Management implication

The best managers in AI-native systems become portfolio shapers and control-system designers, not just task trackers.
